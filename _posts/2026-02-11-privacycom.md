## How Your Bank Became a Snitch
![A wallet full of surveillance tech](/assets/privacycom/walletspy.jpg)

Before 1970's banks usually decided for themselves what information they needed to setup an account for you. In fact, it wasn't uncommon for banks to not require anything more than just your name and your money. 

Any information you did share usually stayed between you and your bank. Banks worked hard to maintain good relationships with their customers, and there was genuine trust.

That all started to change in 1970, marking a pivotal shift in banking—from customer-focused institutions to government compliance machines tasked with compiling dossiers on every account holder.

>Naomi Brockwell provides an amazing analysis of the following timeline in her video ["The Truth About Bank Privacy."](https://odysee.com/@NaomiBrockwell:4/Financial-surveillance:e)
{: .prompt-info }

### 1970: The Bank Secrecy Act

The government began requiring banks to keep detailed records of customers and transactions and required them to report transactions over $10,000 to the treasury department.

When this was challenged in court, the Supreme Court ruled it wasn't a Fourth Amendment issue. Their reasoning? Once you hand information to your bank, it's not yours anymore. It's part of the bank's business records. And the bank can do whatever it wants with it.

### 1992: The Annunzio-Wiley Anti-Money Laundering Act

Banks were now required to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) on anything they deemed "suspicious." And here's the kicker: it became illegal for banks to tell you they filed one. Your bank could report you to the government, and by law, they had to keep it secret.

You wouldn't know. You couldn't contest it. You'd never even find out.

### 1999: The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act

Remember when banks at least pretended to keep your information confidential? This act killed that.

Financial institutions could now sell your data to as many third parties as they wanted. Transaction dates. Amounts. Recipients. Personal details from your application. All of it up for sale.

The only requirement? They have to provide customers with a "privacy notice."

I pulled up Chase's privacy statement. They reserve the right to share my data for seven different reasons, including "For nonaffiliates to market to you."

Translation: *We're selling your information to random companies so they can advertise to you. Thanks for banking with us!*

And this doesn't just apply to the banks, it applies to anyone in the chain of the transaction. 

Consider this: a single credit card swipe involves up to seven different entities. Your bank (Chase). The card network (Mastercard). The store (Target). Point-of-sale systems. The retailer's bank. Mobile wallets. Budgeting apps. Each of these entities can—and does—sell your data.

Many companies claim this is perfectly safe because they anonymize your data before selling it. But [research](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/credit-cards-offer-far-less-privacy-than-you-think/) from MIT shows you can be identified with more than 90 percent accuracy using just four purchases—three if the price is included. And that's after companies "anonymized" the transaction records.

### 2001: The Patriot Act

After 9/11, privacy took another hit. Know Your Customer (KYC) regulations formalized what banks must collect: your name, current address, date of birth, and more. In many cases, you now have to submit a government-issued photo ID just to open an account.

This creates enormous risk. We've already seen a multitude of data breaches where people's photo IDs were spilled onto the internet for anyone to see and abuse.

Opening a savings account shouldn't require the same scrutiny as crossing the border, yet here we are.

### 2021: The American Rescue Plan Act

Any transaction over $600 on payment apps—Venmo, PayPal, Cash App—gets reported directly to the IRS.

Six hundred dollars. That's the threshold. Split rent with your roommate a few times? The IRS knows. Sell some old furniture on Facebook Marketplace? Reported.

Taken together, this system of corporate and government surveillance gives countless entities an intimate look into our personal lives.

Financial transactions aren't just numbers: they reveal your activities, associations, beliefs, health conditions, relationships, political leanings. The most private details of your life, all up for sale.

So how can we fight back and regain some privacy?

## Taking Back Control

The surveillance is overwhelming. I get it. But here's the good news: you can fight back. 

Here are three ways to reclaim your financial privacy, starting today.

### Cash

"Cash is king."

You've heard it a thousand times. And when it comes to privacy? It's absolutely true.

Cash is increasingly being phased out, but it remains the most private way to make a purchase. No digital trail. No banks, card networks, or retailers selling your transaction history.

Of course, we live in a digital world. You can't hand cash to Netflix. But there are workarounds for digital purchases—you can buy prepaid Visa cards or gift cards with cash and use those when a digital payment is required.

### Monero

Let me start with a huge disclaimer: most cryptocurrencies are the opposite of private.

Bitcoin? Ethereum? They run on public blockchains where every transaction is visible to anyone who looks. The amount, the wallet addresses, the entire history—all permanently recorded for the world to see.

But a few cryptocurrencies took privacy seriously from the start, and Monero is the best of them.

Like Bitcoin, Monero uses a public, decentralized blockchain. But unlike Bitcoin, all transaction details are obfuscated—the sender, receiver, and amount are all hidden.

Unfortunately, Monero's success at privacy has made it tricky to obtain. Many countries have pressured crypto exchanges to delist it because it's so hard to trace. In the US, you can still buy it on exchanges like Kraken or through apps like Cake Wallet.

A popular workaround: buy a cryptocurrency like Litecoin on any exchange, then send it to Cake Wallet where you can swap it for Monero. Takes a few extra steps, but it works.

### [Privacy.com](https://privacy.com/): Masked Credit Cards

And this brings us full circle—back to that credit card I showed you at the beginning, and one of my personal favorite tools.

Privacy.com is a credit card masking service that lets you create virtual cards. To the rest of the world, these are real, fully valid Mastercard credit cards. But here's the magic: they're not tied to your identity, and they're incredibly customizable.

You can create two types of cards: single-use and merchant-locked.

Single-use cards lock after one transaction. That's the type I used to open this blog. They're perfect for one-off purchases or trial subscriptions—you don't have to remember to cancel before getting charged again. The card burns itself after the first use. Done.

Merchant-locked cards can be used unlimited times, but only at one specific merchant. Create a Target card, and if Target suffers a breach, criminals can't use that card number anywhere else. It's worthless to them.

You can extend the security of any card by setting spending limits. Cap your Best Buy card at $100 per month, and it'll simply decline any purchase that exceeds your limit.

These are awesome security features. But what I really love is the *privacy*.

When you shop online, merchants require a name and billing address. With a real credit card, you have to provide your actual information or the transaction gets declined. Even if you created an account with a fake name, the moment you pay with a credit card, everyone suddenly knows who you really are.

But Privacy.com virtual cards aren't tied to any identity or address. You can use any name or address you want.

For example, you can create a new account on Amazon, pay for purchases under a completely different name, and still have packages shipped to your real address.

>Warning: Avoid using your real home address as a billing address. Merchants are required to store billing information for seven years. If something is being shipped to your house, only use your real address for shipping—make the billing address something else.

Get creative with names and addresses. Create chaos for the surveillance economy by poisoning their datasets. Suddenly there are multiple names at your address, different names tied to your phone number, or you appear at billing addresses scattered across the country.

>Tip: For digital purchases where nothing physical ships, use an address from a state with no sales tax—Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon, or Alaska. You'll save money and muddy your data trail. I use [fakepersongenerator.com](https://fakepersongenerator.com/) to quickly generate random names and addresses.

Now, Privacy.com isn't perfect. They're subject to KYC regulations, which means they need your real information when you sign up such as your real name and address. If you object or try to provide false information, you'll be declined.

It isn't ideal to submit to more KYC, but given the lifetime of protection you get from Privacy.com, I believe this is a valid one-time compromise.
