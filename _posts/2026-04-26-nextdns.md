## Introduction

In a [previous](https://kryptosis.xyz/posts/rtbsurveillance/) article, we explored how the advertising present in mobile applications is used to fuel commercial surveillance products capable of tracking any smartphone in the country.

Unlike browsers, apps don't let you install an extension to block ads and trackers. With the notable exception of GrapheneOS's network permission toggle, there is no easy way to silence the constant flow of personal data leaving our devices through trackers, ads, and SDKs embedded in mobile applications. What we need is a system-wide solution that blocks ads and trackers across every app at once.

That is where NextDNS comes in.

## NextDNS

NextDNS blocks ads and trackers by intercepting the DNS requests being made by the applications on your device. To understand why this works, let's pause and briefly discuss how DNS functions.

Every app that serves ads or tracks your behavior has to communicate with external servers to do so. Those servers have IP addresses, but IP addresses change frequently, so apps refer to them by human-readable domain names instead, like ad.com or tracker.io.

You actually do the same thing every day without thinking about it. Rather than memorizing a string of numbers to reach Google, you type google.com and your device handles the translation automatically. It does this by sending a request to a DNS server, which looks up the domain and returns the corresponding IP address to your computer.

NextDNS works by acting as that DNS server for your device. When an app tries to reach a known ad or tracking domain, NextDNS recognizes it and blocks the request before it ever connects. We will configure it with a curated list of domains to block, so the protection is automatic and comprehensive.

It sounds more complicated than it is. Let's set it up.

**Setup**

**1. Create your account**

Visit [nextdns.io](https://nextdns.io/) and click "Try it now." To make sure your configuration is saved permanently, click "Sign Up" and create an account. Practice good digital hygiene here: use an email alias and a unique password stored in a password manager.

**2. Configure your blocklist**

Once logged in, navigate to the "Privacy" tab. This is where you select the blocklist NextDNS will use to filter DNS requests. NextDNS includes their own default list, but we are going to replace it with something better.

Click "Add a Blocklist" and search for *Hagezi*. These are my preferred lists: they are robust, well-maintained, and regularly updated. The different Hagezi options vary by aggressiveness — the Light list blocks around 70,000 domains while the Ultimate blocks close to 300,000. It may be tempting to go straight for the Ultimate, but the more domains you block, the greater the chance that legitimate sites break. That being said, I actually *do* recommend starting with the Ultimate and working your way down if you notice problems. These lists are easy to swap, so finding your preferred balance of protection and usability takes only a minute while ensuring you are receiving the maximum amount of protection possible.

>**Tip: View the blocklist:** These blocklists are just long text files that list all the domains NextDNS will block. If you visit https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/hagezi/dns-blocklists@latest/domains/ultimate.txt you will see the blocklist for Hagezi Ultimate.

**3. Connect your device**

Go back to the "Setup" tab and select your device type. Android setup is straightforward. iOS takes a few more steps.

For iOS, you have two options: the NextDNS app or a configuration profile.

For the app, scan the QR code on the Setup tab and install it. You will be prompted to enter your NextDNS ID, which is displayed at the top of the Setup tab. Enable the toggle inside the app, then follow its prompt to go to Settings → General → VPN & Device Management → Restrictions and Proxies and select the NextDNS app.

For the configuration profile, open your browser on your iOS device, go to nextdns.io, and sign in. Scroll down on the "Setup" tab and tap the apple.nextdns.io link. Give the device a name and tap Download. Confirm the prompts that follow, then open Settings. Just below your name you will see a "Profile Downloaded" box — tap it, then tap Install. Enter your passcode and confirm once more. The profile is now active. If you ever want to remove it, go back to that profile in Settings and select "Remove Profile."

**4. Watch it work**

Here is the satisfying part. Return to the NextDNS website and open the "Analytics" tab — you will likely already see activity. Then open the "Logs" tab, which lists every domain your device has attempted to reach. Blocked requests appear with a red indicator.

To see it in action, open a new browser tab and visit a site like yahoo.com. Return to the logs and watch the blocked connections populate. Then open any app on your phone and do the same. The volume of outbound requests most apps make — and how many get blocked — tends to be eye-opening.

**5. Explore the remaining tabs**

The "Security" and "Parental Controls" tabs offer additional settings worth reviewing. The defaults are sensible, but feel free to adjust them to your needs.

The "Denylist" tab lets you manually block specific domains. For example, adding *.facebook.com will prevent your device from connecting to anything on the Facebook domain.

The "Allowlist" tab lets you whitelist domains that are being incorrectly blocked. If a site stops loading, check the Logs tab to see what is being intercepted, then add it here. That said, if you are experiencing frequent breakage, the simplest fix is to return to the Privacy tab and switch to a less aggressive Hagezi list.

**6. Disable logging**

Once everything is configured to your liking, go to the "Settings" tab and turn off Logs. This adds an extra layer of privacy by ensuring NextDNS is not retaining a record of your DNS activity. You can re-enable logging at any time if you need to troubleshoot.

>**Tip: Create Separate Profiles:** At the top of the NextDNS dashboard there is a dropdown menu for profiles. You can create as many as you like. This is useful if you want to install NextDNS on a family member's device but give them a less aggressive filter list than your own — each profile is configured independently.

## Conclusion

Congratulations! You now understand the DNS process and have used it to your advantage to install a system-wide ad and tracker blocker on your device.

When I first set this up, I was absolutely floored by the sheer volume of connections the apps on my phone were making behind the scenes — an invisible system quietly sending vast amounts of personal data to third parties without any consent or acknowledgment. Shedding light on that system is the first step. Acting on it, as you just have, is how we begin to reclaim control of our data and the sovereignty that comes with it.

If you found this helpful, share it with your friends and family. The more people who take these steps, the more we send a message: Privacy isn’t suspicious. It’s not for people with something to hide.
