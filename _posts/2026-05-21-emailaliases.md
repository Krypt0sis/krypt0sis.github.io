## Introduction

It seems like everyone wants your email address these days. Walk into a grocery store or restaurant, and they're nudging you to join a loyalty program — just drop in your email for instant savings! Try to sign up for any online service and you'll hit the same wall: no email, no account. It seems harmless enough, but here are three reasons you might want to think twice before handing over your real one:

1. Spam

    Imagine you create a Target account or a Best Buy account. Almost immediately, your inbox starts filling up with marketing emails. With just one or two stores, it's manageable. But over time, your inbox stops feeling like a place for personal connections and starts feeling like a full-time spam disposal job. The frustrating reality is that once you hand over your real email address, it's very hard to turn off the faucet. Yes, most services offer an opt-out option, but in my experience, plenty of them keep sending emails anyway, regardless of your preferences.

2. Breaches

    Spam is annoying, but data breaches are a far more serious concern. Hardly a day goes by without a new report of some company suffering a breach and spilling customer data online — and that data almost always includes email addresses. Even if you've opted out of marketing emails, companies still hold your address in their databases, leaving it exposed whenever a breach occurs.

    When hackers get hold of email addresses, phishing attacks typically follow. These emails are designed to look legitimate, often prompting you to sign in to your account. In reality they are malicious, directing you to fake login pages built to harvest your password. The goal is simple: password reuse is incredibly common, and attackers know it. A stolen Target password has a decent chance of being the same one protecting something far more sensitive, like a bank account.

3. Privacy

    Using the same email address across every service is quietly eroding your privacy. Companies routinely sell customer data to data brokers, who piece together surprisingly detailed profiles on individuals through a process called linkage analysis. Every time you use the same email, you're adding another tile to a mosaic of your digital and physical life — your shopping habits, your location, your interests, your beliefs.

    Combined with other data streams, brokers can build unsettlingly accurate pictures of who you are and how you think. That information is then sold to advertisers, political organizations, and surveillance operations — often without you ever knowing.

    Luckily, there's a simple solution to all three of these problems: email aliases.

## What are email aliases?

An email alias is essentially a decoy address you hand to a company instead of your real one. Say your real email is jilljane56@proton.me. Instead of giving that out, you generate an alias like shopping.richly533@slmails.com and hand that to the store. When the company emails you, the message goes to the alias and is then forwarded to your real inbox.

But here's the power move: if the spam becomes too much, the company gets breached, or you simply want to cut ties, you just disable the alias. Instantly, you'll never hear from them again! It's a clean, simple layer of protection that addresses all three problems we just covered.
